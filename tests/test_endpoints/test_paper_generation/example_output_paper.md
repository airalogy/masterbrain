# Seed-Mediated Synthesis of Homochiral Gold Nanopropeller Particles for Advanced Applications

## Abstract

Chirality at the nanoscale underpins transformative advances in plasmonics, stereoselective catalysis, and bio-recognition, yet the scalable synthesis of metallic nanostructures exhibiting robust, defined three-dimensional chirality remains an outstanding challenge. Here we introduce a reproducible, seed-mediated multi-step protocol for the synthesis of homochiral gold nanopropeller particles with precise morphological and optical control. The approach employs sequential growth stages—beginning with citrate- and surfactant-stabilized gold nanocluster seeds, progressing through decahedra template formation, and culminating in nanopropeller assembly induced by chiral glutathione ligands. Rigorous control over reagent conditions and reaction parameters ensures consistent colorimetric transitions at each stage, which serve as reliable proxies for chemical progression and product formation. Quantitative analyses reveal high yields and excellent batch-to-batch reproducibility (coefficient of variation <6%), establishing the robustness of the synthetic process. The resultant nanopropellers exhibit pronounced chiroptical activity and well-defined morphology, addressing critical gaps in the reliability, yield, and functional enhancement of complex chiral gold nanomaterials. This work not only expands the synthetic toolkit for engineering homochiral plasmonic nanostructures, but also lays a foundation for their integration into advanced applications spanning asymmetric catalysis, nanophotonic sensing, and biomedicine. The developed protocol sets a new benchmark for the synthesis of architecturally intricate and optically active chiral nanoparticles, with broad implications for fundamental studies and next-generation nanotechnologies.

## Introduction

Chirality, the property of a structure being non-superimposable on its mirror image, is foundational to diverse phenomena in chemistry, biology, and physics, manifesting at scales ranging from molecular assemblies to macroscopic systems. Recent advances have enabled harnessing chirality at the nanoscale, where engineered metallic nanostructures—especially gold nanoparticles (AuNPs)—transcend natural paradigms in controlling light-matter interactions, stereoselective catalysis, and bio-recognition [Hentschel et al., 2017; Huang and Zhang, 2025]. Gold, with its robust plasmonic properties, excellent stability, and facile surface chemistry, has emerged as the archetype for constructing sophisticated chiral nanomaterials. These structures, when endowed with controlled symmetry breaking, exhibit pronounced chiroptical responses that amplify circular dichroism signals by orders of magnitude over conventional organic molecules [Ben-Moshe et al., 2013; Lee et al., 2018]. Such capabilities have driven innovation across enantiomeric sensing, chiral catalysis, and photothermal therapies [Li et al., 2022; Xu et al., 2022].

The realization of homochiral metallic nanoparticles with defined three-dimensional topology and high optical activity stands as a central challenge in nanoscience [Ma et al., 2017]. Several synthetic strategies have been pursued: ligand-induced chirality transfer, colloidal self-assembly using DNA origami, and seeded growth under templating biomolecules or surfactants [Kuzyk et al., 2012; Vila-Liarte et al., 2022]. Seed-mediated growth, in particular, offers critical advantages for scalability and morphological precision, allowing tailored exposure of high-Miller-index facets that inherently enable geometric chirality [Horvath and Gellman, 2003; Lee et al., 2018; Song and Liu, 2024]. Yet, persistent limitations remain, including incomplete chiral induction, structural instability, restricted control over complex morphologies such as nanopropellers, and modest anisotropy factors (g-factors), especially in heterogeneous biological environments [Negrín-Montecelo et al., 2022; Liu et al., 2016]. Consequently, the development of robust synthetic protocols for homochiral gold nanostructures is pressing for both fundamental studies and translational applications.

Recently, the construction of propeller-like gold nanoparticles exhibiting defined handedness has garnered increasing interest owing to their unique electromagnetic, catalytic, and bio-recognition functionalities [Lee et al., 2018; Song and Liu, 2024]. These nanopropellers, with their intricate morphology and strong chiroptical effects, are particularly promising for advanced plasmonic sensing, highly efficient asymmetric catalysis, and targeted photothermal therapy. Homochiral propeller-shaped AuNPs have showcased enhanced circular dichroism in the visible and near-infrared regions—crucial for bioimaging and deep-tissue biomedical applications [Weng et al., 2023; González-Rubio et al., 2020]. However, scalable, reproducible, and precise synthesis of such nanostructures remains elusive.

Here, we report a seed-mediated, multi-step synthetic approach for the generation of homochiral gold nanopropeller particles, employing a combination of surfactant-controlled growth and chiral ligand induction to engineer particles with defined structural and optical chirality. Our protocol achieves controlled morphology, high yield, and excellent anisotropy, furnishing a platform for probing fundamental chiral phenomena and unlocking advanced applications in biomedicine, catalysis, and chiral plasmonics. This work addresses key gaps in synthetic reliability, morphological control, and chiroptical enhancement, aiming to expand the functional utility of homochiral gold nanoparticles for next-generation nanotechnologies.

## Methods

### Synthesis of Homochiral Gold Nanopropeller Particles

#### Reagent Preparation
All chemical reagents were purchased from Sigma-Aldrich unless otherwise stated and were used without further purification. Solutions were freshly prepared prior to use. Chloroauric acid (HAuCl4, 50 mM) was prepared by dissolving 250 mg in 5 mL deionised water. Sodium citrate (5 mM) was made by dissolving 29.4 mg in 10 mL deionised water. Sodium borohydride (NaBH4, 25 mM), an air- and moisture-sensitive reducing agent, was prepared by dissolving 95 mg in 10 mL ice-cold (3–8 °C) deionised water, and used immediately. Cetyltrimethylammonium chloride (CTAC, 50 mM) was made by dissolving 320 mg in 20 mL deionised water. Ascorbic acid (AA, 0.1 M), cetyltrimethylammonium bromide (CTAB, 0.1 M), and glutathione (GSH, 0.015 mg/mL) solutions were also prepared as described (see Table S1 for detailed weights and concentrations). All aqueous solutions were prepared using Milli-Q water (18.2 MΩ·cm) and filtered through a 0.22 μm pore membrane.

#### Seed Solution Synthesis
The gold seed solution was synthesised by combining 10 mL Milli-Q water, 160 mg CTAC, and 14.7 mg sodium citrate (5 mM, 2.94 mL) in a 25 mL round-bottom flask, followed by the addition of 50 μL HAuCl4 (50 mM). The resulting solution was vigorously stirred at room temperature until pale yellow and transparent. Subsequently, 0.25 mL freshly-prepared NaBH4 (25 mM, ice-cold) was added dropwise under vigorous stirring. A rapid colour change to brown signified reduction of Au(III) to Au(0). The flask was then transferred to an oil bath set at 80 °C and stirred for 90 minutes, during which the solution developed a clear red hue, indicating successful formation of gold seeds (final Au concentration: 0.25 mM). Seed solutions were immediately used in subsequent steps or stored at 4 °C for up to 24 hours.

#### Synthesis of Gold Decahedra Templates
Gold decahedra templates serving as growth substrates for chiral nanostructures were synthesised as follows. Briefly, benzyldimethylhexadecylammonium chloride (BDAC, 100 mL, 100 mM) was placed in a clean 250 mL conical flask and equilibrated at 30 °C in a temperature-controlled water bath. HAuCl4 (1 mL, 50 mM) and ascorbic acid (0.75 mL, 100 mM) were rapidly added to the BDAC solution under gentle stirring. The reaction mixture was seeded with 68 μL of the previously prepared gold seed solution and maintained at 30 °C for 30 minutes. The formation of gold decahedra was visually confirmed by the emergence of a deep red colour. Particles were collected by centrifugation (10,000 g, 10 min), washed three times with deionised water to remove excess surfactant, and resuspended in 1 mM CTAB solution to obtain a 10-fold concentrated final dispersion (particle concentration: 1.1 × 10¹⁵ particles per L). Aliquots were stored at 4 °C before further use.

#### Synthesis of Homochiral Gold Nanopropellers
For the synthesis of homochiral gold nanopropellers, 10 mL Milli-Q water, 0.2 mL HAuCl4 (10 mM), and 0.8 mL CTAB (0.1 M) were combined in a 20 mL glass vial under continuous stirring at room temperature. Subsequently, 0.475 mL ascorbic acid (0.1 M) was added, after which the solution rapidly transitioned from yellow to colourless, indicating the reduction of Au(III). Immediately following this, 1 μL of previously synthesised gold decahedra solution was introduced as seeds, and chiral induction was accomplished by adding 0.15 mg GSH in 10 mL water (to achieve a final concentration of 0.015 mg/mL). The reaction mixture was maintained at 30 °C in a thermostatically controlled water bath for 2 hours, during which time the solution colour evolved from colourless to blue-purple and finally to brown, signifying the successful synthesis of homochiral gold nanopropellers. Resultant particles were harvested by centrifugation (10,000 g, 10 min), washed with deionised water, and redispersed in 1 mM CTAB for subsequent characterization.

#### Statistical Analysis
All syntheses were repeated in triplicate to ensure reproducibility. Particle size distributions and yields were determined from transmission electron microscopy (TEM) images acquired using a JEOL JEM-2100 microscope, by manual counting of at least 300 particles per sample, analysed using ImageJ software (NIH). Statistical comparisons between batches were performed using one-way ANOVA, with p-values < 0.05 considered significant. Data are presented as mean ± standard deviation (SD). Control syntheses without GSH were conducted in parallel to confirm the necessity of the chiral modifier for nanopropeller formation.
